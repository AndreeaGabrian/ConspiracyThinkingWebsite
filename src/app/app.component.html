What is QAnon?

A modern-day conspiracy that thrives on mystery, misinformation, and community-driven narratives.

The Spread of QAnon

Trump & Political Influence

As QAnon grew beyond anonymous forums, “bakers” and influencers shaped it around real-world events. Trump and his allies indirectly endorsed QAnon ideas at rallies, online, and even in official statements.

Key Moments:
- Trump suggested using disinfectants like bleach against COVID-19 in 2020.
- QAnon spread false claims about Antifa starting wildfires during the West Coast wildfire season.

Misinformation & Public Impact

QAnon fueled misinformation during global crises, including the COVID-19 pandemic. Some followers promoted dangerous remedies like Miracle Mineral Solution (industrial bleach) as a cure.

Trump helped amplify QAnon’s reach by retweeting over 70 QAnon-linked accounts, some later linked to Russian intelligence operations.

Massive Online Presence:
- 69M+ tweets referenced QAnon between 2017-2020.
- 487K+ Facebook posts spread QAnon narratives.
- 281K+ Instagram posts engaged with QAnon hashtags.

Meme Culture & Symbolism

QAnon thrived on Internet culture, using memes, in-jokes, and cryptic messages to engage followers. Many concepts were borrowed from films like The Matrix (1999) and White Squall (1996).

Followers saw themselves as “red-pilled,” referencing The Matrix, and urged others to “Follow the White Rabbit” (Alice in Wonderland metaphor for discovering hidden truths).

QAnon’s most famous slogan, “Where We Go One, We Go All” (WWG1WGA), came from White Squall.

Tracking the Crumbs

Secret Knowledge

Q would hint at having secret knowledge and make predictions for future events that never actually happened, all written in vague and confusing language.

Leading Questions

Instead of providing real answers, Q mostly asked leading questions, guiding followers to interpret information as they wished.

Hero-Trump Narrative

Over time, the postings changed, becoming more focused on proving the story of Hero-Trump and his far-right political orbit.

Qniverse of Madness

As it became harder to ignore Q’s incompetency, followers started treating Q like a celebrity, using fandom behavior to validate inconsistencies. Constructing an alt-reality using books, fanart, memes, maps, and clocks—visual tools that helped connect the community and justify their beliefs.

Sight plays a crucial role in truth-making as well as fooling. The 1957 BBC broadcast on spaghetti farming deceived many viewers simply by showing a fake activity.

In an era of increased confusion, both anonymous and official figures attempt to use complexity to grow their following—both online and in politics.

From Conspiracy Thinking to Populist Narratives

While QAnon started as an online conspiracy movement, its core ideas—distrust in elites, belief in hidden truths, and a battle between good and evil—align closely with populist ideology.


The movement gradually merged with far-right populism, reinforcing existing anti-government and anti-establishment sentiments. This transition turned conspiracy thinking into a political weapon, fueling division, misinformation, and radicalization.


How does conspiracy thinking reinforce populism? The need for simple explanations, anger-driven beliefs, and blame attribution make conspiracy narratives a powerful tool for populist leaders, helping them rally support and justify extreme actions.

Populism, Far-Right Ideology and Conspiracy Thinking

Populism and Conspiracy Thinking

Core of Populism: Populism is based on the belief in a good, homogenous, unified common people vs. a corrupt elite that exploits them. This strong "us vs. them" mentality makes people more likely to embrace conspiracy theories that reinforce this division.

Conspiracy mindset: Populists tend to seek simple, binary explanations for complex issues. People look for coherent reasoning between complicated events and try to reduce their difficulty to a polar, binary view.

How Misinformation Shapes Populist Beliefs

Belief in simple solutions makes people assume that all events can be easily explained, leading to predictability in an uncertain world. This contributes to the far-right's appeal, as it offers black-and-white narratives to explain crises.

Anti-immigrant sentiment: Many populists experience nostalgia for a “better” past and blame societal decline on immigrants and minorities. People discern a nation's thriving past and downgrade the present due to low-status immigrants and refugees.

Emotional Manipulation & Polarization

Anger is a key emotion in populism. It emerges when people feel their cultural identity is threatened. Anger arises as a result of a threat to cultural identity and feelings of unfairness.

Blame attribution: Populists perceive the government as controlling the economy, causing frustration. When people experience anger, they are more likely to attribute responsibility to an external cause.

Why This Should Worry Us

Polarization & Radicalization: Populist rhetoric encourages blaming others for societal issues, absolving responsibility from those in power. Populists tend to blame others in most problems while absolving the (right) people of responsibility.

Spreading Misinformation: Conspiracy thinking fuels distrust in science and institutions, leading to dangerous real-world actions. The COVID-19 pandemic is an example, where far-right conspiracy theories encouraged people to drink bleach as a cure.

Encouraging Fast Thinking & Stereotypes: Populist ideologies promote superficial, “fast” thinking, making people more susceptible to stereotypes and extreme ideologies. Anger prompts people to follow quick thinking and increases stereotypes as a way to reduce uncertainty.
