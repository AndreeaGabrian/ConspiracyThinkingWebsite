What is QAnon?

A modern-day conspiracy that thrives on mystery, misinformation, and community-driven narratives.

The Spread of QAnon

Trump & Political Influence

Key Moments:

Misinformation & Public Impact

QAnon fueled misinformation during global crises, including the COVID-19 pandemic. Some followers promoted dangerous remedies like Miracle Mineral Solution (industrial bleach) as a cure.

QAnon thrived on Internet culture, using memes, in-jokes, and cryptic messages to engage followers. Many concepts were borrowed from films like The Matrix (1999) and White Squall (1996).

Tracking the Crumbs

Secret Knowledge

Q would hint at having secret knowledge and make predictions for future events that never actually happened, all written in vague and confusing language.

Leading Questions

Instead of providing real answers, Q mostly asked leading questions, guiding followers to interpret information as they wished.

Hero-Trump Narrative

Over time, the postings changed, becoming more focused on proving the story of Hero-Trump and his far-right political orbit.

From Conspiracy Thinking to Populist Narratives

While QAnon started as an online conspiracy movement, its core ideas‚Äîdistrust in elites, belief in hidden truths, and a battle between good and evil‚Äîalign closely with populist ideology.


The movement gradually merged with far-right populism, reinforcing existing anti-government and anti-establishment sentiments. This transition turned conspiracy thinking into a political weapon, fueling division, misinformation, and radicalization.


How does conspiracy thinking reinforce populism? The need for simple explanations, anger-driven beliefs, and blame attribution make conspiracy narratives a powerful tool for populist leaders, helping them rally support and justify extreme actions.

Populism, Far-Right Ideology and Conspiracy Thinking

Populism and Conspiracy Thinking

Populism is rooted in the belief in a good, homogenous, unified common people versus a corrupt elite that exploits them. This "us vs. them" mentality reinforces in-group identity and boosts the appeal of conspiracy theories.

Conspiracy thinking is associated with lower levels of analytical thinking and education. Populists often seek simple, binary explanations for complex issues. They tend to overestimate co-occurring events and perceive intentionality where none exists.

How Misinformation Shapes Populist Beliefs

Belief in simple solutions and black-and-white narratives offers comfort in uncertain times, giving people the illusion of predictability.

Anti-immigrant sentiment: Populists may romanticize a "better past" and blame societal decline on minorities. They often attribute decline to low-status immigrants and refugees undermining national prosperity.

Emotional Manipulation & Polarization

Anger is central to populism, especially when cultural identity feels threatened. Anger emerges from perceived unfairness and cultural threats.

Blame attribution: Populists often believe that external forces like the government are at fault. When angry, people are more likely to assign blame to external causes.

Why This Should Worry Us

Populism and far-right ideologies start to spread all across the world, even in countries with a strong liberal history. How can you spot them? Pay attention to the following characteristics.

Polarization & Radicalization: Populist rhetoric encourages blaming others for societal issues, absolving responsibility from those in power. Populists tend to blame others in most problems while absolving the (right) people of responsibility.

Spreading Misinformation: Conspiracy thinking fuels distrust in science and institutions, leading to dangerous real-world actions. The COVID-19 pandemic is an example, where far-right conspiracy theories encouraged people to drink bleach as a cure.

Encouraging Fast Thinking & Stereotypes: Populist ideologies promote superficial, ‚Äúfast‚Äù thinking, making people more susceptible to stereotypes and extreme ideologies. Anger prompts people to follow quick thinking and increases stereotypes as a way to reduce uncertainty.
